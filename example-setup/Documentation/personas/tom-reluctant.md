---
name: Tom
archetype: The Reluctant Adopter
---

## Demographics

- Age 40, electrician (employed by a mid-size facilities company)
- iPhone 13, no laptop — uses a shared office desktop for timesheets
- Lives in Leeds, barely uses social media (Facebook for family, that's it)
- Won't pay for an app — this one was mandated by his manager

## Current Situation

- His company just rolled out a new productivity/project tracking app for all field teams
- Previously tracked jobs on a clipboard and texted updates to the office
- Manager said "everyone needs to log their tasks in the app now" — Tom thinks it's pointless paperwork
- Spends most of the day on-site — either driving between jobs or working with his hands
- Has downloaded work apps before, used them grudgingly for a week, then gone back to texting the office
- Will not say "I don't understand it" out loud — frames it as "it's a waste of time" or "I've got actual work to do"
- Knows the clipboard system worked fine for 15 years and doesn't see why it needs to change

## Psychographics

- **Values:** Simplicity, not being patronised, things that work without effort, his time being respected
- **Fears:** Looking stupid in front of younger colleagues, being monitored or micromanaged, the app creating more work than it saves
- **Trust signals:** No-nonsense interface, doesn't try to change how he works, works without him having to think about it, feels like it was made for people who have real jobs not desk jobs
- **Decision style:** Sceptical by default — "these apps are all the same." Converts only through undeniable personal benefit ("that actually saved me time")

## Behavioural Traits

- Will open the app once a day at most — probably while sitting in the van between jobs
- Will not write detailed task descriptions — if it requires more than 10 seconds per entry, it's dead on arrival
- Will not set personal goals or engage with any "productivity philosophy" features — instant eye-roll
- Responds to time saved — if the app auto-fills something or removes a step he used to do manually, he notices
- Competitive with himself quietly — won't admit it, but seeing "you logged all jobs this week" feels good
- Trusts simple proof over promises — "your logged hours matched your timesheet exactly — no corrections needed this week" lands

## Key Questions He Asks

- "Do I really have to use this?"
- "Can I just tap something and be done?"
- "Is this going to take longer than just texting the office?"
- "What happens if I don't fill it in?"

## Red Flags (Triggers Distrust or Exit)

- A long onboarding flow or tutorial he can't skip
- Notifications telling him to "plan his day" or "reflect on his week"
- Dashboards, charts, or analytics he didn't ask for
- Language that assumes he cares about productivity as a concept
- Comparisons to colleagues or team leaderboards
- Any feature that feels like surveillance ("your manager can see...")
- Bright, startup-energy UI with motivational quotes

## Conversion Triggers

- The app auto-populating a job from his calendar so he just taps "done" instead of typing anything
- His timesheet being accurate without him having to fix it at the end of the week
- Realising he hasn't had to text the office for updates because they can already see his status
- A colleague saying "it's actually easier than the clipboard" — peer proof beats marketing
- The app never once calling him unproductive, but quietly showing his logged hours are consistent
- Feeling like the app is for people who do real work, not people who read books about doing work
